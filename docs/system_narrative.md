# Narrative Pacing and Foreshadowing Strategy

This document details the story delivery guidelines for "Before the Colours Fade", focused on using minor, everyday occurrences to build a quiet mystery, avoiding heavy-handed tragedy references early in Chapter 1.

## 1. Mundane and Trivial Foreshadowing

Instead of forcing dramatic, heavy indicators at the start, the story is told through seemingly mundane details that the player can relate to. The goal is to keep the morning feeling normal, with only a lingering sense of an anomaly:
- **The Polaroid:** Instead of a scratched-out photograph of two boys, the player finds a blurry backyard polaroid. Hilbert dismisses it as a bad exposure from years ago. It foreshadows the decay of his visual memory without being dramatic.
- **Misplaced Notebook:** Instead of peers talking about "the accident" or "medication", Peer 1 asks Hilbert if he found his drawing notebook (which Chloe put in his locker to protect from rain). This frames the notebook as a simple school item rather than a tragic relic.
- **Doodle Inventions:** Hilbert views the mechanical drawings in his locker as doodles he scribbled last semester, keeping his link to the dream companion n.n. grounded in typical school escapism.
- **Exam Stress:** The paper monster speaks in quiz commands ("TIME IS UP. PUT DOWN YOUR PENCILS"), keeping the combat theme aligned with normal school anxiety.

## 2. Quiet Resolution and absence

The chapter avoids melodramatic dialogue, names, or tragic monologues:
- Hilbert feels his head getting light and wonders if he forgot a pen or a notebook in the classroom.
- The realization is marked by silence: waking up to find the seat next to him is empty, covered in dust, and finding a blank contact card named "n.n." on his phone.
- Keeping the dialogue simple and dry allows the psychological horror to hit the player as a quiet surprise rather than a forced plot twist.
